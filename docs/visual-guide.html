The one-paragraph version

What Tradewind is

Home-services businesses — plumbers, landscapers, fencing companies — run on Jobber, a CRM whose funnel is Request → Quote → Job → Invoice. Their two recurring headaches: inbound leads slip through the cracks, and customer records are messy (duplicates, half-filled). Tradewind sits on top of Jobber as a sharp assistant: it reads each new lead and the customer attached to it, ranks it by how worth chasing it is, flags duplicate or incomplete records, and prepares a grounded reply — then hands the work to the owner to sign off. Think "prepared and waiting for approval," not "a bot let loose on the business."

How it's built

The WAT architecture

The system deliberately separates thinking from doing — that separation is what makes it reliable. If one AI tries to do every step, small errors pile up. So judgment lives in one layer and exact execution in another.

The instructions

Workflows

Plain-language playbooks describing each task, step by step.

The decision-maker

Agent

The AI. Reads the workflow and makes the judgment calls — "is this likely a duplicate?", "what should the reply say?"

The execution

Tools

Small, predictable scripts that do the exact, repeatable work — reads, sheet writes, rules.

The Knowledge base — the only source of facts

The Agent may state a price, service, or policy only if it comes from owner-confirmed knowledge. If a fact isn't there, it escalates the question to the owner instead of guessing.

Built in layers, safest first

The layer progression

Each layer is proven before the next is added. Everything built so far is read-only, and nothing has run on real customer data yet — it's validated against a test sandbox and clearly-labeled sample data.

Why it's trustworthy, not reckless

The safety design

Trust is built into the structure, not bolted on. Four guarantees hold at every step.

Least privilege

The Jobber connection asks for read-only permission. There is no send/message capability anywhere in the code.

Never invents facts

Every customer-facing claim must come from confirmed knowledge. A built-in grounding gate blocks any draft that cites an unconfirmed fact.

Approval gate

Drafts and proposals are surfaced with their sources and an approve / edit / reject control. The system never overwrites an owner's edits.

Sample can't be sent

Demo knowledge is tagged as sample; drafts built on it are flagged so they can never be sent as if they were real, confirmed facts.

What's next

Roadmap

Our first real market-validation interview — with a business in the fencing trade — flagged that these owners are capacity-constrained, not lead-starved ("lots of customers, can't take them all"; "most Facebook leads are trash"). That pointed straight at lead scoring — now built, as the Score & Prioritize layer above. Here's what remains.

1

Real-data onboarding read-only

The read-only pipeline — read · score · draft — is proven on a test sandbox and labeled sample data. The next milestone is a first run against a live Jobber account, where the owner tunes the scoring weights and confirms the knowledge base with real facts.

2

Layer 3 — Execution planned

Approved drafts get sent and approved cleanups get applied in Jobber — the first capability that can change data or reach a customer, and therefore the most carefully gated and the last to ship.
